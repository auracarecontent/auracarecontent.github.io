DAY 7

Night Snacks & Poor Sleep

🌜

Why Late-Night Eating Feels Comforting — but Costs You Rest

After a long day, your brain wants relief, not calories.
Cortisol stays high, blood sugar may be low — so you reach for something sweet or crunchy to relax.
But eating close to bedtime makes your body focus on digestion instead of repair.
✨ The comfort is real — but the timing is wrong.
🧠

What Happens in Your Body After Late Snacks

When you eat at night, blood sugar and insulin stay elevated for hours.
That blunts melatonin, the sleep hormone, and delays deep-sleep cycles.
High insulin also blocks growth hormone, which normally handles overnight repair.
Meanwhile, cortisol stays too high — keeping your body in "day mode."
💡
Result: lighter sleep, slower recovery, and more cravings next day.
🕰️

The Science of Eating Rhythm

Your metabolism follows a circadian clock: active by day, slow by night.
Late eating confuses that rhythm, signaling that "daytime" hasn't ended.
Finishing dinner at least 3 hours before bed helps glucose drop naturally and improves sleep quality.
🥗

How to Break the Night-Snack Habit

✅
Have balanced dinners. Add protein, fiber, and magnesium-rich foods.
✅
Don't skip breakfast or lunch. Under-eating early fuels evening hunger.
✅
Hydrate after dinner. Thirst often feels like hunger.
✅
Set a kitchen curfew. Choose a finish time and stick to it.
✅
Wind-down ritual. Herbal tea, stretching, journaling — teach your brain it's bedtime.
🧪

Science Spark

Eating within a 10-hour daily window (for example 8 AM–6 PM) helps lower nighttime cortisol and improve recovery.
Time-restricted eating trials show better glucose control, lower evening cortisol, and improved sleep quality compared with late-night eating.
📚 Nature Communications 2020; Cell Metabolism 2019.
