Why You Sometimes "Can't Catch Your Breath"

When anxiety rises, breathing becomes shallow and trapped in the chest.
Carbon dioxide builds up, your heart races, and the brain reads that as danger ‚Äî keeping the stress loop active.
The physiological sigh breaks this loop by quickly restoring oxygen balance and sending a powerful "all clear" signal through the vagus nerve.
This reflex is automatic in animals and children ‚Äî every time you cry or yawn, your body performs it to reset the nervous system.

How It Works

A physiological sigh consists of two quick inhales through the nose ‚Äî one deep, one shorter ‚Äî followed by a long, slow exhale through the mouth.
The double inhale opens tiny air sacs in the lungs, increasing oxygen and pressure sensors' feedback to the brainstem.
The long exhale activates the vagus nerve, slowing the heartbeat and lowering cortisol.
The result: a measurable drop in stress within 1‚Äì2 minutes.
