The Science of the Gut‚ÄìInflammation Loop

Your gut lining is protected by a thin layer of cells that renew every few days. Stress, poor diet, alcohol, and lack of sleep can weaken that barrier.
Once it's "leaky," immune cells activate and raise inflammation markers like CRP and IL-6 ‚Äî both linked to fatigue, anxiety, and higher cortisol.
Restoring gut calm isn't about extreme diets ‚Äî it's about rhythm: steady meals, colorful fiber, hydration, and at least one slow, peaceful moment daily.
Inflammation is your body's way of saying: "I'm working too hard ‚Äî please slow down."

What About the Vagus?

The vagus nerve is your body's main inflammation regulator ‚Äî it acts like a "brake" that quiets immune overactivation.
When vagal tone is low, the gut becomes more reactive, the barrier weakens, and inflammation signals rise, fueling fatigue and irritability.
But when the vagus is gently activated, it sends an anti-inflammatory message through the whole body, helping calm cytokines and supporting gut repair.
A stronger vagus means steadier energy, fewer crashes, and a gut that can finally "turn down the volume."
