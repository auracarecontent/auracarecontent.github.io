Daily Focus

Your stomach feels stress long before your mind admits it.
When pressure builds, your body releases cortisol ‚Äî the main stress hormone that prepares you to act but also diverts energy away from digestion. Blood flow to the gut slows, stomach acid drops, and food stays longer than it should. That's why anxiety can feel like nausea, bloating, or a tight knot inside.

The Science of the Cortisol Loop

Cortisol and your gut communicate constantly through the vagus nerve. When cortisol stays high, it reduces vagal tone ‚Äî the calming signal that helps your gut relax and digest.
Low vagal activity means slower digestion, higher inflammation, and more "nervous belly" sensations.
But here's the good news: cortisol also listens to the body. When you calm your breath, move gently, or eat slowly, cortisol starts to fall within minutes.
This is how the gut and brain rebuild trust ‚Äî one calm signal at a time.

What About the Vagus?

The vagus nerve is your body's built-in antidote to cortisol ‚Äî it sends the "slow down" signal that brings digestion back online after stress.
When vagal tone is low, the stomach stays tense, digestion slows, and cortisol keeps rising, creating a loop where stress feeds gut discomfort and gut discomfort feeds more stress.
Strengthening the vagus through slow breathing, steady meals, and gentle movement breaks this cycle, helping your gut shift from alert mode to recovery mode.
A calmer vagus means a calmer stomach ‚Äî and a cortisol rhythm that finally starts to settle.
