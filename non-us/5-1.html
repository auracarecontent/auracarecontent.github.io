Why Stress Affects Your Gut So Fast

When your nervous system is stuck in "fight-or-flight," blood flow is redirected from the stomach and intestines toward the muscles and heart.
The brain tells the gut to pause digestion ‚Äî just in case it needs to run from danger.
This makes sense in emergencies but not in modern life, where stress is constant.
Over time, this keeps the vagus nerve underactive, leading to:
  • slow digestion or bloating,
  • poor nutrient absorption,
  • abdominal tension or cramps,
  • and unpredictable appetite.
To restore calm digestion, you need to reactivate the vagus ‚Äî the "rest and digest" command line.

How the Vagus Keeps the Gut‚ÄìBrain Link Alive

The vagus nerve sends two-way signals between your brainstem and intestines.
It senses when you're stressed and when your gut is ready to digest.
When vagal tone is strong, peristalsis (the gut's natural wave movement) is steady, and enzymes are released efficiently.
When it's weak, communication slows and you feel "stuck" both mentally and physically.
Gentle twisting and deep breathing help massage the organs, increase blood flow, and send rhythmic pressure changes that directly activate vagal fibers in the abdomen ‚Äî resetting the system.
