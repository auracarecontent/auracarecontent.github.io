Meet Your Vagus ‚Äî The Nerve of Calm

It starts in the brainstem and travels down to your heart and digestive organs.
When you slow your breathing and lengthen your exhale, pressure receptors in your lungs and chest send calming signals through the vagus nerve back to your brain ‚Äî lowering heart rate and cortisol.
This process, called respiratory sinus arrhythmia, is a direct reflection of vagal activity.
High heart-rate variability (HRV) ‚Äî the small, healthy changes between heartbeats ‚Äî means your vagus is active and flexible.
Low HRV means your stress brake is weak.

Why the Vagus Nerve Matters

The vagus nerve forms the main branch of your parasympathetic nervous system ‚Äî the "rest and digest" mode that restores calm after stress.
When vagal tone is strong, your body quickly recovers from tension:
  • Your heart rate stabilizes,
  • Digestion flows smoothly,
  • Sleep becomes deeper,
  • Focus and mood improve.
When tone is weak, you stay in a constant "on" state ‚Äî tense, wired, and fatigued.
Strengthening vagal tone is like fitness training for your nervous system. Each slow breath is a rep.
