The Science of Sodium and Sugar

Salt (sodium) isn't the enemy ‚Äî your body needs it to move electricity through nerves and muscles.
But modern diets overload it, especially through processed foods and sauces.
When sodium builds up, water follows it into your bloodstream, increasing blood volume and pressure.
At the same time, high sugar intake causes insulin and adrenaline surges that tighten blood vessels and drop HRV.
If this happens daily, your nervous system stays in a subtle state of alert ‚Äî heart rate rises, arteries lose elasticity, and even mild stress feels amplified.

Why It Matters

Too much salt, not enough hydration, and sugar spikes make your vagus nerve less responsive.
This "silent overdrive" weakens your natural relaxation reflex.
Rebalancing minerals and glucose helps your body recover that rhythm:
  • Lower sodium = less water retention and vessel stiffness
  • Balanced hydration = better oxygen and heart rhythm
  • Stable glucose = smoother vagal tone and energy levels

Science Facts ‚Äî Electrolytes, Sugar, and Stress

Reducing sodium by 1,000 mg/day (half a teaspoon of salt) can lower systolic BP by 5‚Äì6 mmHg (He et al., 2020).
Dehydration increases heart rate and cortisol within minutes (Armstrong et al., 2016).
Glucose swings trigger sympathetic activation and inflammation even in non-diabetics (Ceriello et al., 2012).
Balanced electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium) improve HRV and blood vessel relaxation (Stanhewicz & Kenney, 2015).
You can't calm your mind if your cells are thirsty. Hydration, balanced minerals, and stable blood sugar keep your nervous system grounded and your heart pressure steady.
