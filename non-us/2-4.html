Why Stress Affects Your Gut So Fast

When your body stays in "fight-or-flight" mode, blood moves away from your stomach and intestines to your muscles and heart ‚Äî as if you need to run.
Your brain tells the gut to pause digestion until things feel safe again.
But when stress happens all the time, your body never fully switches back.
As a result, the vagus nerve ‚Äî which keeps digestion calm ‚Äî becomes less active.
You might notice:
  • bloating or heaviness after meals,
  • stomach cramps or tightness,
  • irregular appetite.
To help your digestion work smoothly again, you need to turn the vagus nerve back on ‚Äî your body's natural "rest and digest" mode.

How the Vagus Keeps the Gut‚ÄìBrain Link Alive

The vagus nerve sends two-way signals between your brainstem and intestines.
It senses when you're stressed and when your gut is ready to digest.
When vagal tone is strong, peristalsis ‚Äî the gut's natural wave-like movement ‚Äî stays steady, and enzymes (special proteins that help break down food) are released efficiently.
When it's weak, communication slows and you feel "stuck" both mentally and physically.
Gentle twisting and deep breathing help massage the organs, increase blood flow, and create rhythmic pressure changes that directly activate vagal fibers in the abdomen ‚Äî resetting the system.
